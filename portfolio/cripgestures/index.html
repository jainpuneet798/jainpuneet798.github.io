Crip Sensorama: Speculating the metaverse with (and for) the disabled (Ars Electronica * IT:U)

This is a work-in-progress, to be exhibited at IT:U and Ars Electronica as part of FOUNDING LAB program.
"The progression of AI-driven devices and virtual environments has made verbal communication and oral commands the primary mode of interaction with technology. From infancy, individuals are accustomed to using voice commands for nearly every aspect of their lives. Basic tasks such as turning on lights, ordering groceries, controlling transportation, and operating household appliances are all accomplished through verbal directives.

As a result, the reliance on manual dexterity or physical movement for operating gadgets, devices, or machinery has greatly diminished. Keyboards, touchscreens, and physical controls have become obsolete relics of the past. The convenience and efficiency of voice-activated systems have led to a gradual decline in the development and utilization of fine motor skills related to manual manipulation.

Jobs that previously required physical dexterity, such as surgery or assembly line work, now heavily rely on advanced voice-controlled robotic systems. Surgeons of this future era are trained not in the precision of hand movements but in the mastery of vocal commands to guide highly precise surgical instruments.

However, as society becomes increasingly reliant on oral communication, there arises a need for individuals with disabilities or conditions affecting speech and mouth movements to share their expertise. People with diverse abilities and experiences in navigating a world where oral communication is paramount become invaluable resources. Their insights and adaptability in utilizing alternative communication methods or technologies for daily tasks become crucial in this future society.

In this speculative future, the over-reliance on mouth movements for interaction and control of technology may lead to a scenario where the broader population becomes less adept at physical manipulation but increasingly proficient in oral communication and command-based interaction with the digital world."

Drawing on queer disabled writer Leah Lakshmi Piepzna-Samarasinha’s provocation: “The Future is Disabled” (2022), this is a collaborative future co-constucted by me and Chat-GPT where voice-controlled devices have superceeded every other technology and efficient micro-gestures of the mouth become a necessity to drive the world. A world where one has to learn from the disabled communities and my collaborators, disabled artist/musician/painter/technologist Eric Desrosiers and Christian Bayerlein to “crip” the micro-pixels of their face-skin.
